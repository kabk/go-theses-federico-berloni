Seppia explores rhythm as a living structure for attention, exchange, and embodied presence. Moving from typographic cadence toward bio-rhythmic response, the project asks whether pulse, timing, and physiological alignment can organize perception before language fully arrives. It is an inquiry into listening, into the body as regulator, and into what kind of relational technology becomes possible when systems activate through presence rather than through command.

I. Introduction

1.1 Suspension, Pre-Verbal Rhythm, and the Return to Listening

A body suspended under water. Salt against the skin. Light filtering from above. Weight dissolving. Sound is no longer sharp. It is rounded. Carried. Ssshhhhhhh... through density. In suspension, the body does not interpret. It aligns.

Before language, there was rhythm. Before intention, before effort, before explanation — there was a system larger than us regulating our growth. Inside the womb, we were not readers of text. We were listeners. Movement. Circulation. Heartbeat. A silent choreography conducted by pulse.

Rhythm preceded words. It structured time before meaning was articulated. It carried information before symbols were learned. It regulated the body before the body learned to regulate itself.

Under water, we remember this. In silence, we remember this. This project begins from that memory — not as nostalgia, but as inquiry.

If rhythm regulated us before language, what role does it still play within systems that are now dominated by text, interfaces and screens? Can we design for listening again?

1.2 From Typographic Structure to Embodied Cadence

I spent the last years crafting systems to formulate letters, words, sentences. I studied spacing, proportion, structure. I learned that a typeface is not only a collection of forms; it is a rhythm distributed across a page.

Typography is controlled cadence. Kerning regulates distance like breath regulates speech. Line length controls tempo. Repetition creates familiarity; variation triggers attention.

Reading is not only visual — it is rhythmic. But the deeper I went into letters, the more I encountered something beneath them.

A man once said: “The deeper I go into science, the more religious I become.” The deeper I go into letters, the more I return to rhythm.

Perhaps communication does not begin with symbols. This project does not abandon typography. It abstracts an underlying regulator.

If typography is engineered rhythm, what happens when that rhythm is meant to be a living one?

II. Research Context

2.1 Engineered Rhythm and Filtered Communication

Typography stabilizes language through measured relationships. Spacing, proportion, repetition, and variation regulate how the eye moves across a page and how thought unfolds across time. Every typographic system proposes a rhythm for the reader — a structured path that shapes perception.

This rhythm is deliberate and fixed. It makes meaning portable, repeatable, consistent. It ensures that language can travel beyond the instability of speech and emotion.

Yet embodied rhythm behaves differently. Under strong emotion, syntax hesitates, breath interrupts grammar, repetition replaces clarity, and cadence overrides structure. Biological rhythm reasserts itself beneath linguistic order.

Today, communication increasingly occurs not on blank pages but within platforms. Messages exist independently of the bodies that wrote them. Profiles persist even when their owners are absent. Interfaces structure tone, timing, and visibility through notifications, algorithmic sorting, and social conventions.

Expression becomes filtered — not only linguistically but behaviorally. We adapt to the rhythm of the platform. We respond to signals from screens rather than signals from bodies. Communication shifts from relational presence to interface compliance.

When technology can exist without human presence — when a profile remains active regardless of whether the person behind it is there — interaction risks becoming exchange with abstraction rather than encounter with embodied rhythm.

This project emerges from that tension. If communication has become increasingly mediated and stabilized, can embodied rhythm offer an alternative organizing principle for exchange? Not to reject technology, but to explore whether systems can be designed around live presence rather than static representation.

III. Research Question

Research Question

As a type design student, I learned that form shapes meaning. Structure regulates interpretation. The more precisely rhythm is engineered, the more seamless reading becomes.

But what if rhythm does not only support language? What if it can structure experience directly?

This research asks: Can rhythm operate as language — not replacing words, but hosting meaning before and beyond them?

More specifically, this project investigates whether real-time visualization of one’s own heartbeat alters bodily stillness and attention, whether shared cardiac translation correlates with perceived moments of connection, and whether replacing typographic organization with bio-rhythm, influence behavior.

Three hypotheses guide the exploration.

IV. Hypotheses

H1 — Alignment Through Visibility

When rhythm becomes visible or audible, alignment and attention are triggered independent of verbal instruction. Real-time visualization of one’s own heartbeat increases stillness and sustained gaze.

H2 — Presence Through Shared Bio-Rhythm

If two people’s interaction is driven by bio-rhythms rather than screens, presence may replace passive engagement. Shared heart rhythm translation produces moments of perceived connection between participants.

H3 — Behavioral Shift Through Living Structure

If we pause typographic structures and allow bio-rhythms to organize space, behavior and interaction patterns may change.

If our technology can exist without human presence — like an Instagram profile that remains active— then we risk interacting with artificial forms detached from embodied reality.

“What happens if technology only activates through presence?”

These hypotheses remain grounded within the installation.

V. Theoretical Groundings

5.1 Physiological-Artistic-Relational Grounding

In the seventeenth century, William Harvey described the heart as measurable movement, transforming it from symbolic center into circulatory regulator.

Rhythm became anatomical fact rather than metaphor. This physiological grounding situates the heart as dynamic system — a continuous oscillation sustaining life.

Contemporary discourse around cardiac alignment, such as that promoted by the HeartMath Institute, proposes correlations between emotional states and heart rhythm variability.

While such claims are not adopted uncritically here, they demonstrate ongoing cultural interest in synchronization and physiological alignment.

In artistic practice, Lisa Park has translated internal biodata into sound and vibration, allowing bio-signals to shape spatial experience. Her work demonstrates that physiological data can become aesthetic structure.

This project extends that inquiry by asking not only how biodata can be represented, but how it can organize relational interaction between participants.

The concept of Memoria Cordis, articulated historically by Ibn Arabi, frames the heart as a site of knowing beyond speech. Here, this idea is approached as cultural articulation rather than metaphysical claim, pointing toward embodied cognition that precedes linguistic formulation.

Similarly, biomimicry as articulated by Janine Benyus suggests learning from adaptive natural systems rather than imposing rigid mechanical control.

Together, these references support a shift from command-based design toward responsive and relational systems.

VI. Methodology

6.1 Practice based Research

The installation developed for this research, titled Seppia, functions as both artwork and research instrument. It consists of spatial design, heart-rate sensors, sound sources, and devices arranged in what is supposed to feel like a soft-cozy environment where the body becomes central.

The system activates only when participants are physically present and connected to sensors. Without bodies, the installation remains quiet. Presence becomes the trigger.

Heartbeat signals are captured through sensors and translated into digital code, which modulates light intensity and sound frequency in real time. There are no prerecorded loops and no fixed sequences. The rhythm is live, fluctuating with the participant’s physiological state.

When two participants connect simultaneously, their rhythms are put in space together, allowing patterns of convergence and divergence to become visible and audible.

Observation focuses on behavioral indicators such as stillness, gaze duration, breathing adjustments, and verbal reflections following participation.

Material experimentation played a significant role in development. Repeated 3D printing failures revealed how minor structural misalignments destabilized the entire form. Calibration became analogous to kerning in typography — micro-adjustments determining overall coherence.

In coding, even slight latency disrupted the sense of immediacy; timing proved more important than visual complexity. Rhythm demanded precision.

Participants often became physically still when confronted with their own pulse translated into sound. Some unconsciously synchronized their breathing; others maintained extended eye contact during moments when rhythms converged.

Interaction shifted from task-oriented behavior toward sustained attention. Each observation led to iterative adjustments in sensor sensitivity, sound frequency, and signal modulation. The project evolved through listening — not only to participants, but to the system itself.

6.2 Observation and Documentation

Observation focuses on:

• Duration of stillness

• Sustained gaze

• Breathing adjustments

• Verbal reflections after participation

• Moments of synchronization

The methodology is qualitative and practice-based. It does not claim universal measurement. It documents behavioral tendencies and experiential reports.

VII. Relational Technology

8.1 From Interface to Exchange

This project proposes a form of relational technology. Instead of instructing users through buttons and menus, the system responds to presence. Technology becomes mediator rather than commander.

Biodata functions not as information in the traditional sense, but as shared signal. No message is composed, no content transmitted. What is exchanged is timing — pulse as relational field.

In contemporary communication environments, expression is shaped by algorithmic visibility and social performance. (IG) Emotional states are compressed into predefined formats.

As interaction becomes increasingly screen-based, physiological presence recedes. We often encounter representations rather than rhythms.

The risk is not technology itself, but disembodiment.

This research offers a counter-experiment.

“What if technology required our pulse?”

“What if systems activated only through presence, refusing autonomy without the body?”

“What if communication began from shared tempo rather than typed message?”

From typographic rhythm to bio-rhythm, from engineered cadence to adaptive presence, this project places living structures in dialogue.

It does not reject language; it investigates whether rhythm can host meaning before and beyond it. Under water, the body listens. Perhaps design can listen too.
