SUSPENSION

Perception, Rhythm, and the Question of Contact

A body suspended under water.

Salt against the skin. Light filtering from above.

Weight dissolving.

Sound softened and carried through density.

In suspension, the body does not resist—it aligns.


› Movements slow down, breath pauses, and the surrounding becomes less something to observe and more something to feel. Under water, the body becomes aware of space and the pulse becomes audible in the quiet blue.

What the body encounters is adaptation, sensing how its rhythm meets the rhythms around it. Under water, the body listens. In this research communication is approached not only as linguistic exchange but as an embodied process shaped by presence and timing.

For years my work revolved around tools of communication: writing systems, visual arrangements, and digital interfaces. I consider them powerful because they can freeze meaning and allow it to travel across time and distance, as IG profiles host our identities regardless of our presence and our momentary states. Over time it became increasingly evident that such systems capture only a fraction of what humans express when they encounter one another.

Through this research I explore how interactions could be shaped before speech appears, supported by quiet processes that unfold alongside words; posture shifts, breathing adjusts, attention gathers or dissolves. These bodily signals rarely survive digital translation, yet they are fundamental portions of exchange. They create a condition in which communication unfolds before it is spoken; they set the tone, the rhythm, and the quality of presence that language later enriches.

And so:

"Did we forget something when we designed our social tools?"

or

"Can we rely on social media to consolidate our relations?"

These questions become the starting point of a design investigation. Through the development of a device called Seppia, I explore how biorhythms (the complex of systems that our body activates in response of stimuli before logical process takes place) specifically the heartbeat, can act as a medium of meaning between people.

In this exploration I look to position myself as a designer working on mediation between body and technology, asking what can be tracked and expressed where words fall short.

This research therefore emerges from a tension. Rather than rejecting social tools, I am interested in how they can be reoriented; what if these tools could engage with presence itself, with timing, with the subtle adjustments that occur between bodies in proximity? Sensors and live tracking suggest real time information while polished profiles suggest representation.

Seppia emerges from this line of thinking. It is not designed to transmit meaning in a conventional sense, but to make perceptible a process that is already present in the body; in doing so, it proposes a different entry point into communication, where alignment is not forced and misalignment is not a failure, where emotions are perceived and not represented, where shifts and continuous oscillations suggest dynamism in our dialogues.

RHYTHM

Body as an Adaptive System and Perceptual Medium

› As mentioned before, our body is a recipient for a multitude of coexisting systems, processes and cycles, that often reacts to our surrounding before logic comes into place.

Among these processes, rhythm, cadence and coordination plays a central role. The body operates through continuous cycles; breathing, circulation, neural activity, that adjust in response to both internal states and external conditions, we just need to think about how the heartbeat rises and softens in response to intensity changes, continuously adjusting as our experience unfolds. These rhythms are not stable or mechanical; they fluctuate, forming a biological sys-tem that remains responsive to its environment. Rather than being fixed patterns, they are adaptive, constantly negotiating between what is happening inside the body and what is encountered outside of it.

According to HeartMath Institute, a non-profit research organization founded in 1991, the heartbeat is one of the most immediate expressions of these systems. It does not follow a perfectly regular tempo, but accelerates and decelerates depending on emotion, activities and environment stimuli. Even in moments of stillness, it reflects subtle changes in the body’s condition. It registers shifts that often occur before conscious logic; anticipation, tension, calm, or hesitation, and carries them as variations in rhythm. Because of this, the heartbeat occupies a unique position among our internal systems.

Research on heart rate variability explores these fluctuations, showing how the interval between heartbeats changes continuously rather than remaining constant. Studies conducted by the HeartMath Institute suggest that certain patterns of variability are associated with different physiological states, such as calmness or stress. While these interpretations are still debated within scientific contexts, what remains relevant for this research is not the classification of emotional states, but the recognition that the HRV is subject of variations and responses that can be tracked and perceived.

However, in this investigation, the core does not lie in measuring or decoding fixed values out of this rhythm. We observe what happens when heartbeat becomes something that can be encountered directly, without representation into language or numbers. This shift from interpretation to perception is crucial, because it changes the role of the body from something to be analysed into something that participates in experience.

This inquiry finds resonance in artistic practices that explore how invisible processes can be made perceptible. A valid example can be Lisa Park, a Korean American performance and installation artist known for using biofeedback technology to turn invisible human emotions into physical experiences. So physiological signals such as brain activity are translated into vibrations that affect physical materials. In her installation "Eunoia", electrical signals captured through EEG sensors are converted into sound frequencies that cause water to ripple; a conceptual overlap to our approach to the invisible yet made perceptible signal. The surface of the water becomes a visible trace of internal states, allowing observers to witness changes in emotion as movement in space.

The viewer does not read the signal; they perceive it as rhythm.

A different but related approach appears in the work of Ryoji Ikeda, a Japanese artist currently based in Paris, considered a pioneer in contemporary electronic music and audiovisual installations and world renowned for his focus on the raw characteristics of sound, light, and data. Ikeda translates large scale scientific data into immersive audiovisual environments. In installations such as “Data-Verse”, information from astronomical systems is rendered as rapidly shifting sequences of light and sound. The viewer is not asked to interpret the data in a conventional sense, but to experience it as intensity, frequency, and rhythm; again a similar form of inquiry where black boxes are unveiled and sensed.

In this context, data is no longer something to be understood through cognition alone; it becomes something that is felt and sensed in proximity. Across these references, a shared movement emerges: a shift from representation toward perception. Internal processes: whether biological, material, or computational, are brought into a space where they can be felt. This shift is important because it suggests that communication can occur not only through symbols and language, but through shared conditions and oscillating signs. Within this context, the trajectory of this research becomes more precise.

How a specific bodily rhythm, such as the heartbeat, can be used to mediate interaction between people if it is made perceptible in real time. The aim is not to replace language, but to explore a layer of communication that operates alongside it. This is where practice becomes central. Rather than answering this question theoretically, the research moves into the construction of a device that allows this condition to be experienced directly.

DEVICE

From Invisible Rhythm to Perceptible Interaction

› Seppia is deeply informed by the aesthetic and sculptural explorations of Arnaldo Pomodoro, the Italian artist born in 1926 in Morciano di Romagna, whose work is marked by a continuous dialogue between interior and exterior forms, and by a fascination with ancient writing systems. This vision is rooted in early experimentation with metal casting techniques, for example the one with cuttlefish bones, whose lightweight aragonite structure was traditionally used in jewellery and small scale metal manufacture, as a methodology that transfers the bone patterns to the metal surface, which Pomodoro later engraves to unveil cuneiform writing systems.

The relationship between interior and exterior informs the interactive system. Inspired by this exploration of layered forms, the device makes an inner physiological process measurable, transmittable, and perceptible. The heartbeat is tracked, processed, and reencoded as sound, establishing a system where biological signals are no longer scientific black boxes.

The device consists of two handheld units, each equipped with a pulse sensor, a microcontroller, and a bone transducer. When a participant holds the device, the sensor detects variations in blood flow through the skin, allowing the system to identify the timing of each heartbeat. This signal is then processed and transmitted to the paired unit, where it is reproduced as sound. Each participant hears the heartbeat of the other. The system does not display data or provide interpretation, nor does it translate the signal into visual information or numerical values. Instead, it preserves the temporal quality of the heartbeat, allowing it to be experienced directly as rhythm. The interaction is therefore not based on understanding, but on perception. Participants are not asked to interpret what the rhythm means, but to remain with it as it unfolds, allowing their attention to get triggered on a new level.

A key aspect of the design is that the device only functions when both participants are physi-cally engaged. Without contact, the system remains inactive. This dependency ensures that the interaction cannot occur without presence. The device does not simulate connection; it requires it. In this sense, it shifts the role of technology from an independent system to a mediator device that only exists through use. The device is not something that delivers content, but something that creates a a new condition for dialogues.

The development of Seppia was not linear. It unfolded through a series of iterations in which technical constraints and experiential qualities continuously informed one another. The pulse sensor required careful calibration to detect subtle variations in blood flow while remaining stable across different users. Skin contact, pressure, and positioning all affected the quality of the signal, meaning that the physical design of the device had to support consistent contact without forcing a rigid grip.

The form has to invite holding, not prescribe it.

At the same time, the electronic system required refinements. The raw signal produced by the sensor contains noise: irregular fluctuations that do not correspond to actual heartbeats. Filtering was necessary to isolate the peaks that indicate each pulse. However, this process introduced its own challenges. If the signal was over processed, the heartbeat became too regular, losing the subtle variations that make it feel alive. If it was under processed, the output became unstable and difficult to follow. The final configuration was therefore a balance between clarity and variability, allowing the rhythm to remain recognisable while preserving its natural irregularity.

One of the most critical aspects of the system was latency. The perception of a heartbeat depends on immediacy. Even a small delay between detection and playback can alter how the rhythm is experienced. When latency is noticeable, the signal no longer feels connected to the body that produces it. It becomes detached, more like a recording than a live process. For this reason, significant attention was given to reducing the time between input and output. This involved optimising both the code running on the microcontroller and the communication between the two devices. In practice, achieving low latency was about maintaining a continuous temporal link between two bodies.

The interaction enabled by Seppia can be understood through two modes: direct and indirect. These modes are not predefined by the system but emerge through how participants engage with it.

In direct interaction, participants consciously respond to the rhythm they hear. They may attempt to synchronise their breathing with the heartbeat, adjust their posture, or focus their attention on aligning with the perceived tempo. This mode is characterised by intention. Participants are aware that they are engaging with another person’s physiological rhythm, and they actively try to relate to it. In some cases, this produces a form of coordination, where breathing patterns begin to follow the rhythm being heard.

In indirect interaction, the response is less conscious. Participants do not necessarily attempt to align yet changes still occur. Breathing slows gradually, movements become more contained, and attention stabilises. These shifts happen without explicit decision, suggesting that the body is responding to the rhythm at a level that precedes conscious control. This mode became particularly significant during testing, as it revealed that the device does not require instruction to generate response. The condition itself is sufficient to influence behaviour.

Testing sessions were conducted in controlled yet open settings, where participants were invited to engage with the device without detailed explanation. This allowed observation of how interaction unfolded naturally. Several recurring patterns emerged. Many participants became still shortly after hearing the other person’s heartbeat. The initial moment of contact often produced a pause, as if attention was being recalibrated. This stillness was not imposed; it appeared as a response to the presence of the rhythm. Eye contact also played a role in the interaction. In many cases, participants maintained longer periods of gaze than would typically occur in everyday situations. The combination of auditory rhythm and visual presence created a layered interaction, where participants were simultaneously aware of each other’s bodies and rhythms. This often led to a sustained attentional state that did not rely on verbal exchange. Breathing was another observable factor. Some participants reported consciously adjusting their breathing to match the rhythm they heard, while others showed gradual changes without reporting awareness of doing so. This distinction between conscious and unconscious adaptation reinforces the idea of direct and indirect interaction. The device does not prescribe how participants should respond; it creates a condition in which multiple forms of response can emerge.

• Duration of stillness
• Sustained gaze
• Breathing adjustments
• Verbal reflections after participation
• Moments of synchronization

Moments of synchronisation were observed, but they did not occur as perfect alignment. Instead, they appeared as temporary phases in which rhythms became closer, then drifted apart again. This fluctuation is important, as it suggests that the interaction is not about achieving uniformity, but about engaging in a continuous process of adjustment. The relational dynamics between participants remain adaptive rather than fixed.

Verbal reflections collected after the interaction often pointed toward a sense of presence that was difficult to describe. Participants spoke about feeling connected, aware, or attentive, but struggled to articulate exactly why. This gap between experience and description is central to the project. It indicates that the interaction operates in a space that is not fully captured by language, reinforcing the initial question of the research. From these observations emerges the central hypothesis of Seppia: that shared biorhythms can create a form of interaction that precedes and supports language by establishing a com-mon temporal ground between participants.

In this way, the project suggests a shift in how technology might be designed. Rather than creating systems that operate independently of the body, it proposes systems that depend on it. Rather than focusing on content, it focuses on relation. The updated version of Seppia, currently in development, continues this exploration by refining both the technical system and the conditions of interaction, preparing the device for a second public exhibition in which these dynamics can be observed over longer durations and with a broader range of participants.

CONCLUSION

From Engineered Mirrors to Adaptive Presence

› Contemporary communication technologies are highly effective at transmitting information, yet they often reduce the complexity of interaction by separating it from the body. Messages circulate independently of presence, emotional expression adapts to algorithmic visibility, and interaction is shaped by formats designed for mere consumption and circulation; IG profiles are a perfect example of representation that lacks body presence while live streams, probably the biggest trend in new generations’ digital consumption, simply cannot capture effectively what reciprocal interaction in presence can host and this is why I believe that breathing, hesitation, anticipation, and silence rarely survive digital translation in our nowadays social tools.

Communication became faster, but it also became thinner.

Seppia does not reject spoken language or technological mediation. Instead, it asks whether technology might be designed to listen differently; to amplify the rhythms already present between bodies and so having a new level of exchange in support of our words.

This research therefore proposes a simple question.

“What if social tools required our presence?”

Under water.

Pressure gathers on the skin.

Gravity softens.

The body listens.

Perhaps design can learn to do the same.

A naturally occurring physiological cycle regulating bodily functions such as heartbeat, breathing, or neural activity.

03 Proximity

Physical closeness between bodies enabling subtle forms of interaction and mutual influence.

04 Fluctuation

Continuous variation within a system indicating responsiveness rather than mechanical regularity.

05 HeartMath Institute

A research-based nonprofit, primarily focused on stress reduction and emotional resilience through heart-brain synchronization.

06 Heart rate variability (HRV)

The variation in time intervals between heartbeats, commonly used to observe adaptive physiological regulation.

07 Physiological state

A measurable condition of the body associated with patterns of activity such as calmness, stress, or activation.

08 Arnaldo Pomodoro

Italian sculptor known for bronze works revealing internal mechanical-like forms through fractured external surfaces.

09 Pulse sensor

A device that detects variations in blood flow through the skin to measure heart activity.

10 Microcontroller

A compact computing unit that processes input signals and controls system behavior.

11 Bone transducer

A device that transmits sound as vibration through the bones of the skull rather than through air.

12 Presence-dependent system

A system that only functions when users are physically engaged and co-present.

13 Direct interaction

A mode of engagement in which participants consciously respond to perceived signals.

14 Indirect interaction

A mode of engagement in which responses occur without conscious intention or awareness.
